## Introduction

In a recent class at Yale SOM, a classmate said something interesting in response to a question from BCG Managing Partner Leora Kelman about when the last time she went along the traditional marketing funnel: "when I don't know anything about [x]"

In that moment, I realized that the primary two theories of the consumer journey funnel are actually symbiotic and intuitively linked. 

## Two Models of Consumer Decision-Making

### The Traditional Marketing Funnel
The conventional wisdom presents a linear journey:
- Awareness → Interest → Consideration → Purchase → Evaluation → Loyalty

This model assumes active consumer deliberation—researching options, comparing features, evaluating alternatives. But behavioral psychology research has long told a different story.

### The Salience-Based Model
As I learned from my time last semester with the Yale Center for Customer Insights, behavioral psychologists and behavioral marketing researchers have demonstrated that salience of an idea or heuristic in the mind allows the consumer to expend less mental load, and drives a large fraction of purchase intent.

Whether they be so called: "low-neuron" products or not, salience and context-association is a powerful driving factor in how consumers behave in practice.

Under the behavioral psychology model, the real purchase decision is driven by:
- Stimulus occurring
- Salient association/s triggers instantly
- Purchase happens directly without any active mental effort (or as little as possible)

### What Connects the Models (or Disconnects Them)

Latent salient associations (either with events or ideas) actively pre-empt the entering into the deliberate funnel, because they convince the consumer that they know enough to make a decision. If you were going to buy an expensive watch for the first time, there's a good chance you would go to a Rolex store based solely on their market positioning and advertising. If you saw something you liked while you were there, you would just buy it. Your confidence in the latent psychological associations between Rolex and quality would pre-empt you ever entering the traditional journey funnel.

## The Corona Example: Two Paths to Purchase

Let's contrast how the same beach beer purchase plays out under each model. Imagine you are a consumer on a day at the beach.

### Path A: The Salience-Based Journey

**Level 1 - Active Seeking and Presented With Corona Idea**
> Server: "We have Modelo, Corona, Bud Light, Guinness..."
> Consumer: "I'll take the Corona."

When presented with the brand relative to alternatives, the beach-Corona association has the highest salience. You hear the options but the decision was already made before they finished listing them.

**Level 2 - Category-Prompted Association, Brand Idea Arises Without Prompting**
> Server: "Would you like a beer?"
> Consumer: "Sure, do you have Corona?"

The category cue (beer) triggers the brand association. You needed prompting to think of the category first, but once you do, Corona surfaces immediately.

**Level 3 - Context-Triggered Category + Brand Association**
> *Arrives at beach*
> Consumer: "Man, I'd love a Corona right now."

The situational cue (beach) directly triggers the brand without any category prompting. The association is strong enough that context alone activates it. No one had to ask if you wanted a drink.


**Notice what didn't happen:** Across all of these scenarios, there was no meaningful search where other brands had a chance to compete. There wasn't ever a real comparison or evaluation. The "purchase decision" was made by accumulated associations, not active deliberation.

### Path B: The Traditional Funnel Journey (When Salience Fails)

Now imagine someone at the same beach who *doesn't* have any strong beer-brand beach-beer or beach-beer-brand associations:

**Awareness Stage**
> Server: "Would you like to drink?"
> Consumer: "Hmm, I'm not sure. What do you have?"

They're looking for a drink, but no specific category or brand comes to mind. Now we are actively in the scouting phase, actively evaluating types of beverages. 

**Consideration Stage pt: 1**
> Server: "Beyond water, we have beer, soda, and various juices." or "Our happy hour specials are written here for beer"
> Consumer: "What would you recommend?" or "A Beer might be nice."

Note that even here, there needs to be some sort of baked in preference to opt into a particular choice outside water. Even in the most active, traditionally modeled version of the interaction, there must be at least a category level association to a particular instance. In the case of beers, a server would be unlikely to steer you towards a beer unless they were to volunteer a particular special or event that was strongly associated.

**Consideration Stage pt: 2**
> Server: "We have Modelo, Corona, Bud Light, Guinness, some local IPAs..."
> Consumer: "What would you recommend? I usually drink wine, so I'm not sure what's good." or "What's good on draft?"

By asking, they're actively building a consideration set and seeking trusted sources (the server's recommendation) in order to narrow options. Then they transition into the evaluation stage.  

**Evaluation Stage**
> Consumer: *Thinks between Corona or a Modelo, decides that a Corona will be lighter on calories* 

Note that I added an active attribute comparison here, but the association of Corona being low calorie was not backed by any menu related indicator. They rarely are, these sorts of implicit beliefs are often not explicitly signaled anywhere at the time of decision-making. That said, it appears from the outside to be a rational deliberation using trade-offs against their goals.

**Purchase Stage**
> Consumer: "I'll have a Corona."

A decision is finally made, through supposedly active cognitive effort.

**The Critical Difference**

In Path A, the consumer experiences the decision as obvious and effortless. In Path B, the consumer experiences the decision as uncertain and requiring work.

Consumers always choose path A when it is available to them, because consumers are cognitively lazy. As a result, Path A consumers never entered the funnel. Path B consumers worked through every stage because no brand had built sufficient salience for this occasion.

## What Happens When Salience is Weak

My classmate's observation clarified everything: **"I only go to the funnel when I don't know anything."**

The traditional marketing funnel is purely secondary. It activates only when:
- You lack strong brand associations for the category
- No brand comes to mind for your specific occasion
- and then there is a trigger which creates uncertainty and forces active deliberation

Once that happens, you go search, or AI, or to trusted sources, but this is always necessarily a tiny fraction of true potential consumer demand. If Corona's advertising has done its job, no one should ever get to the traditional funnel. Instead, the associations are so salient that reflexive awareness of uncertainty never occurs.

## First Fast Response

On the flip-side of the journey are the absolutely ingrained associations. Research on "First Fast Response" brought to my attention by Leora Kelman in class can help show the opposite side consumer journey. 

When you combine a scenario with a category and ask for the first brand that comes to mind, you get an instant association that reveals consumer's ingrained wiring:

- "You're on a beach, drinking what beer brand" → Corona
- "You are on a run, what brand are your shoes?" → Nike  
- "you are driving a sportscar in Italy, what brand is it?" → Ferrari

These instant associations don't happen by accident. They're the result of sustained, cross-contextual saturation advertising that builds occasion-based linkages strong enough to bypass conscious effort. BCG's early research suggests that FFR is 2.6 times more responsive at picking up changes in brand association after campaigns than the traditional awareness metrics. See publicly available article [here](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/a-new-metric-to-measure-marketing-effectiveness). 

## Mass Market Advertising as Cognitive Capture

This dynamic between the two versions of the funnel creates a phenomenon that is remarkably similar to regulatory capture.

### The Regulatory Capture Parallel
Regulatory capture occurs when industries gain such influence over their regulators that the decision-making apparatus itself becomes contained within the industry's sphere. Politicians don't actively choose to favor certain companies, instead they're unable to escape the weight of accumulated influence and relationships.

### Cognitive Capture in Consumer Markets
Mass market advertising creates the same containment:

- Consumers don't actively evaluate and choose Corona at the beach
- They literally cannot escape the associative weight
- The decision-making apparatus (System 1 thinking) is captured
- Alternative evaluation never occurs because alternatives never surface to consciousness, or if they do (as in the case of menu options) they never really penetrate into active consideration.

Just as regulatory capture prevents politicians from thinking differently about an industry, salience-based marketing prevents consumers from thinking differently about a purchase occasion. Notice how the power dynamics and responsibilities have shifted though. 

Under traditional regulatory capture, the public theoretically holds the power to prevent the agent (regulators) from being captured, and they can implement solutions through collective action by-proxy to ward against it.

In advertising "capture", there's no external "agent" that consumers can reform because they are the "captured" entity. Consumers must implement solutions by-proxy that ward their own mental associations, and opting out of information has costs (missing relevant product information, paying more than necessary, etc...). More on these challenges in the societal solutions section later.

## What This Means for Marketing Strategy

This analogy reframes the purpose of most advertising, and introduces possible hints towards "solving" any potential societal concerns implicit in this advertising saturated world:

**Traditional View:** Move consumers through the funnel from awareness to purchase, and so we target each level with tailored information that brings them to the next journey stage.

**Salience View:** The goal shouldn't ever be to win the consideration phase. It's to capture the entire decision-making apparatus and prevent consideration from happening at all. Build such strong occasion-based associations (or attribute-based associations) that consumers never enter the funnel. Win every low-frequency purchase with mass-consumer salience.

**Areas of Future Research for Solutions:** If you find the idea of latent subconscious programming as potentially worrisome as I do, then the analogies to regulatory capture may suggest ways for us to break out of this decision-making trap. 

For one, it seems unlikely that we will ever be able to truly re-wire the sub-conscious mind to erase commercial associations without irreparably damaging the core of our opinions and experiences. That said, we can look to some possible solutions:

- **Disclosure and regulations around advertising framing:** By mandating the structure and form of advertising, perhaps we could mitigate its impact on the associations of the subconscious mind. This would be similar to rules that limit the impact of corporate rule-making within government.

We could also reduce the number of avenues where it is acceptable to advertise. By limiting supply side inventory, we might be able to reduce the number and strength of latent associations, and help establish more conscious consumer decision-making. 

- **Reducing Cognitive Load:** In our complex world, consumers are constantly reverting to heuristics as a way to decrease cognitive load. Bombarded with attributes and tradeoffs, we look for easier solutions and rely on our latent associations. Perhaps there is some way we can standardize or simplify choice across a smaller selection of attributes or axes-of-competition. This may help to free up cognitive space for true evaluation across fewer attributes.

- **Widespread Education:** If this status-quo were to become ubiquitously known by consumers, then perhaps market forces would neutralize each other in a mass competition for brand and category associations. Educated consumers could better recognize the effective conditioning as it is occurring, and give themselves counter-programming or counter-weights to the latent advertising spend of companies.

## Conclusion

The traditional marketing funnel still exists, but it represents a failure mode from the ideal marketer's strategy. It's what happens when brands haven't built strong enough salience. 

Most actual purchases look like Corona at the beach: an instant, unreflective association between context and brand. No search, no comparison, no funnel.

Mass market advertising isn't about persuading consumers to choose you after careful deliberation. It's about making deliberation unnecessary. It's about building such heavy associative weights that your brand surfaces automatically when the occasion arises.

The question isn't whether this approach works. It clearly does. The more interesting question is what it means when so many of our choices aren't really choices at all. How do we regain conscious control, if we ever had it in the first place?
